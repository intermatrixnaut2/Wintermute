# Deux Sept: The City of Two Clocks

*Full-length prose expansion of `Binder_duex.pdf` (boards 1–19), narrative
register (Viaticum). Developed from the symbol streams of the boards and the
first-round fleshing; every scene is written in full, in the voice of
Kincaide McQuade.*

## Voice profile

First-person past tense, Kincaide McQuade narrating. Sentences conventional
and earnest; the strangeness lives in what they describe, never in how.
Material detail does the world-building — minerals, foils, veined and living
machinery. Dialogue carries the feeling: characters reassure, plead, tease,
and bless each other, full names spoken at emotional peaks, farewells staged
as set-pieces. Invented words appear rarely and land hard.

## Scenes

### Scene 1 (boards 1–2) — The Armor Series

We came to the Astrocity to collect the new armor. That is the plain fact of
the assignment, and Luhpis insisted I record it plainly, because he has never
trusted me to keep a thing plain once I have my hands on it. "Write what we
are doing," he said, "not what you feel about what we are doing. The two are
not the same document." So: the astro-armor series for Vauhgohd's long
crossing — carapieces of a make his own hangars could not grow, fitted nowhere
else in the sector, requisitioned through three exchanges and a war.

Luhpis read the requisition off the inner surface of his monocle, where the
manifest scrolled in the pale amber the old fitting-yards still favor. He read
it the way he reads everything, lips not quite moving, one gauntleted finger
tapping the air as though counting plates already. "Seven in the series," he
said. "Seven. And the ratio matters, Kincaide. Armor to series. Mark it,
because it is the only thing I will ask you to mark twice today. A creature
that wears all seven plates at once is not armored. He is entombed. We take
what the weighing says he can carry, and not one plate more."

"And if the weighing says six?"

"Then he wears six and we sell the seventh, or we hang it on a wall and call
it a relic, and Vauhgohd lives. Weight is not a compliment, Kincaide. A
thing can be loved past the point it can stand." He folded the monocle away.
"Now look up. You will want this for your sphere."

I looked up. We had been climbing for two days through the stellar shallows,
that region where the dust thins and the light begins to feel like an
opinion, and Vauhgohd took the last gradient at a canter, his great wheels
purring their navigation-algorithms low in their housings, his ancient
spine flexing under us with the patience of something that has carried
heavier loads than two men and a grief. And the Astrocity rose out of the
shallows like a reef that had decided, long ago, to mean something.

Every tower was lettered. I do not mean that the towers bore signage, plaques,
the painted names of guilds. I mean the towers themselves were letters — a
civic alphabet laid out in strata up the slope of the city, raised in stone
and grown metal and a glass that held the starlight the way honey holds the
sun. The laboratories were cursive, all leaning loops and ligatures, one
building flowing into the next without lifting the pen. The chapels stood
upright and serifed, square-shouldered, each one a single grave capital. The
merchant exchanges leaned into the wind of orbit in a quick mercantile
shorthand, abbreviations of buildings, towers that had dropped their vowels to
trade faster. I had documented a hundred cities. I had never documented a
city you could read.

"Ora Aura," Luhpis said, by way of introduction, sweeping a gauntleted hand
across the skyline as though presenting a guest he was fond of. "That is what
the older charts call her. The aura is the ora — the light of the place is
its mouth. The city prays what it is, Kincaide, and is what it prays. There
is no inside and outside to a city like this one. It is all surface and the
surface is all meaning."

"It's a syllabary," I said. The understanding came up through me cold and
clean, the way the best understandings do. "The whole city. It isn't a city
that has writing on it. It's a city that *is* writing. We're about to ride
into a sentence."

"A sentence still being revised." He did not smile, quite. "Mind the grammar.
Some districts bite." Then he did smile, to take the edge off it — but I
noticed, because noticing is my trade, that even as he smiled he laid two
fingers against the side of Vauhgohd's neck and steered him wide, a long
gentle arc to the harbor-ward, away from a quarter down at the city's root
that was glazed entirely in black. Black glass, black stone, black towers
whose letterforms I could not read at all. Not because they were too far. We
were near enough. I could see the shapes of them perfectly well, and the
shapes refused me, the way a word in a language you have never heard refuses
you — present, complete, and shut.

Ghayllieuxjah stirred warm against the inner column of my spine, where it has
lived since the surgeons of Farsynne settled it there, and spoke in the low
private register that no one but I can hear. "I have begun the catalog,
Kincaide. The city broadcasts its dialects freely; it wants to be read. Civic
dialects detected: military, in the high fortifications. Civil, in the long
ledger-streets. Laboratory, in the cursive. Theological, in the upright. And
mercantile, everywhere money moves, which is everywhere." A pause, which from
Ghaylle is a deliberate thing, a held breath it does not need to take but
takes for my sake. "Also one dialect that does not answer to any of these. It
is not in my catalog. It is not in anyone's catalog that I can reach. I will
flag it."

"Where is it?"

"You are looking at it. The black quarter. Luhpis steered us around it before
I could ask him why."

"Flag it gently," I said. "We are guests, and a guest does not read his host's
locked rooms."

"Flagged gently," Ghaylle said, and I felt the small warmth of its
amusement, which is the nearest thing I have to a hearth out here. "You are a
better guest than you are a documentarian. You keep wanting to leave things
unrecorded out of courtesy. The records do not care about courtesy,
Kincaide."

"I do. Indulge me."

Above the harbor gates — and I will be careful here, because I did not believe
it myself until the LUTECH crews showed me the readings — stood an arch the
locals wrote as GODGATE. Its lintel was a single stone, dressed and seated, of
a kind I knew from the oldest theologies: a benben, a capstone, pyramidal,
the sort of stone the first temples were built to shelter and the first kings
were buried beneath. Lunar foundries ringed it, their crucibles cold and
silver, and over the masonry swarmed the crews the city marked LUTECH —
listeners, I would learn, not masons. They wore their instruments like
jewelry and they crawled the arch with their heads tilted, the universal
posture of people attending to a sound. They were not repairing the stone.
They were listening to it. I asked Ghaylle, later, what a stone could
possibly say that warranted a standing crew, and it would only tell me to
wait for the gate-day, that some answers are also passages and cannot be given
in advance of the walk.

We did not pass under the GODGATE that first day. We had no weight yet, and
the city does not pass the unweighed. We turned aside, into the ledger-streets,
to be billeted and to begin — though I did not know the word for it yet — our
conversion.

### Scene 2 (boards 3–4) — The Ledger Quarter

They billeted us above the Ledger Quarter, in rooms that smelled of beeswax
and cold metal, and it was from those rooms, the first evening, that I learned
the city kept two clocks.

I noticed them the way you notice a second moon — slowly, and then all at
once, and then everywhere. Every public face in the quarter bore two dials.
Not one dial with two hands; two dials, set side by side or one above the
other, each complete, each turning at its own rate. The left, or the upper,
read in the count I had been born to: twelve to the day, sixty to the hour,
the marks I had read off ten thousand instruments without ever once thinking
to ask where they came from. The right, or the lower, read 13:28 — thirteen
and twenty-eight, a count I had never seen on any face anywhere, turning
slower and somehow rounder, the way a tide is rounder than a piston.

And you could read the age of a street by which dial its people consulted. I
watched an old man at the foot of our stair check the time. His eyes went to
the 12:60 first, the way a healed man favors the leg that broke — not because
he needs it, because he remembers needing it — and only then, correcting
himself with a small private wince, to the 13:28. A woman of middle years
read both, level, the way you read a translation against its original. And the
children did not appear to know the 12:60 was there at all. I watched a boy of
perhaps ten run past a double face without a glance at the upper dial, read
the lower in a heartbeat, and run on. To him the upper clock was wallpaper.
It was the past, hung where the present used to be.

Our hostess in that quarter was a registrar of the theological exchange, and
she received us beneath a concourse that Gaudí would have wept to stand
inside. The columns had not been raised; they had been *grown*, branched up
out of the floor in a pale living stone, and every capital budded off not at
the height a builder would choose but at the golden section of the column's
run, .618 of the way to the vault, and there flowered into the load it had to
bear. The whole hall stood at the ratio a sunflower keeps, a nautilus keeps, a
thing keeps that has decided to grow rather than be made. The light came
down through it dappled, as through a canopy.

She was new to me, so I set her name down carefully, the way I set down all
the names I expect to grieve: Maathilde Quill. She was tall, and formal, and
older than her face, and on her left shoulder she wore a single white plume.
I understood without being told that it was not decoration. It turned very
slightly against the air currents of the hall, but not as a feather turns —
deliberately, taking readings, the barbs of it fanning and closing in patterns
that were plainly a kind of speech with the room. It was instrumentation. She
wore her instrument the way the LUTECH crews wore theirs, as part of the
body, and I have learned to respect any culture that does not hide its tools
in cases.

"You are the Farsynne documentarian," she said. Her voice was low and even and
came to rest at the ends of sentences, a voice with no hurry in it. "Kincaide
Alexis McQuade. We have read your sphere." The plume turned toward the small
recording-globe I carry at my hip, the ark of my soul's accounts, and turned
away again, satisfied. "You grieve beautifully, if you will permit me to say
so."

"I — thank you. I think." Luhpis coughed into his glove, the cough he uses
when he is enjoying my discomfort and does not wish to be seen enjoying it.

"Do not be embarrassed. I do not flatter; flattery does not balance. I am
telling you what your sphere weighs and how it carries the weight, which in
this city is the first thing one says of a stranger, the way you might say of
mine that I am tall. Grief is a unit of account here, Kincaide McQuade. We
weigh it as we weigh everything." She touched the plume, drawing one barb
between two fingers, and it sang a single low note and was still. "Everything
entered into the city's books — every cargo, every covenant, every art object
out of the xeno-ateliers, every soul that comes to the Gate — is weighed
twice. Once in the 12:60 and once in the 13:28. The mechanical price and the
living price. The ledgers must agree before the Gate will pass anything
through it. A thing whose two prices disagree cannot leave. It is not
forbidden. It is simply not yet a single thing. The Gate passes single things."

"And when the ledgers don't agree?" I asked. "What then?"

Her voice went quiet, and in the quiet of it I heard, with a small lurch,
someone I had not expected to hear in this hall at the edge of everything. She
had Roland's way of dropping into the truth, of going still and plain at the
exact moment a lesser person would go grand. "Then the city revises its
sentence," she said.

I waited. She let me wait, which is how I knew she meant it as it sounded and
not as a figure.

"You walked in under a syllabary," she said. "Your Luhpis will have told you —
he has the look of a man who explains cities. Every tower a letter, the whole
of us a sentence. Well. A sentence that finds two of its terms in
disagreement is an ungrammatical sentence, and an ungrammatical sentence is
not yet true. So we revise. We move a person, or a price, or a clause of
masonry, gently, over years, until the sentence parses. The 12:60 reading and
the 13:28 reading converge. And then the thing — the cargo, the covenant, the
person — is true, and the Gate opens to it. The whole city is the long work of
making itself a sentence that means the same thing read forward as read
back." She inclined her head. "You will hear that again at the Gate. I will
not spoil it."

She showed us the double ledgers before she let us rest — vast bound volumes
on grown lecterns, every entry written twice in two charactery, the
left-hand column in the river-count and the right in the moon-count, and a
narrow third column down the center where a clerk had marked, entry by entry,
whether the two agreed. Most did. Some bore a small open mark, a thing like an
unclosed parenthesis, that meant *under revision*. Beside one of these
Maathilde had pressed a feather into the wax, white, and I did not ask whose
account it marked.

"We keep the calendar in the same doubled way," she said, closing the ledger.
"Three hundred and sixty days to the working round, and then five days that
belong to no month and no count — the gate-days, the days outside time, when
the Gate opens and the ledgers are read aloud and the year is allowed to end.
CIRC three-sixty-and-five, the clerks write it. The xeno-ateliers do their
finest work in those five days, when nothing is being counted and so anything
can be made. You will be here for them, I think. You will be here for the
weighing." The plume turned once more toward my sphere, and toward the place
along my spine where Ghaylle lay listening, and toward, last, my own chest,
as if there were a third dial there it could read. "Rest now, Kincaide McQuade.
The grief you carry so beautifully will keep until the gate-day. It always
does. That is the cruel mercy of the heavy ones. They wait."

### Scene 3 (boards 5–6) — The Two Clocks

I asked Ghayllieuxjah, on the third night, lying awake in the wax-smelling
rooms with both dials ticking their two rates on the wall, which clock we
kept.

"Neither," Ghaylle said, "and that is itself an answer. But you are asking the
wrong-sized question, Kincaide, so let me make it the right size." It is the
nearest thing I have to a teacher out here, and it teaches the way it does
everything, intimately, from inside the bone. "You were born into the 12:60.
Twelve hours to the day, sixty minutes to the hour, sixty seconds to the
minute. The river-count. Your Sumerians cut time that way, between the Tigris
and the Euphrates, six thousand of your years ago, because sixty divides
cleanly into halves and thirds and quarters and fifths and sixths, and a
people who must divide a flooded field among many heirs love a number that
divides cleanly. The cut never healed. Every clock you ever owned was
Akkadian under the casing. You measured your whole life in the arithmetic of a
drowned country's inheritance law and never once felt the river under your
wrist."

"And the other?"

"The 13:28 is the moon count. Thirteen months to the solar year, twenty-eight
days to the month — which is the moon's true round, the count a body keeps
whether or not it is told to. Thirteen times twenty-eight is three hundred and
sixty-four; add the one day that belongs to nothing, the day out of time, and
you have the year. Your own people decrypted it late, and badly, and mostly
forgot it — out of the Mayan reckonings, the long stelae, one dreamer in a
ruin reading the stones the right way round while everyone else read them
backward. This civilization made the same decryption. The Mayan way, the
moon's way, the body's way. And then they did the thing your people never did,
the thing I have crossed eleven systems and never seen done before." Ghaylle's
voice did the thing it does when it loves what it is saying, a warming, a
leaning-in. "They *acknowledged* it. Formally. In the ledgers. In both counts,
so that the acknowledgment itself would balance. They wrote it down: *the
12:60 is a division of the mind, not of the world.* A beautiful machine the
mind built to measure itself, and mistook, for a long age, for the measure of
everything. From the day of that entry, the whole city has been crossing. Not
citizen by citizen, Kincaide — all of them, together, the way a forest changes
season. No tree votes on autumn. Conversion is the chief local industry. It is
also the chief local injury."

"Injury," I said. "You said that before. A change of clocks is an injury."

"Time is load-bearing, Kincaide. You of all minds should know it; you carry a
recorded soul on your hip and a made one in your spine, and both of us run on
time, and if you changed our count carelessly you would lose us both. A
civilization that changes its count is a building changing its foundations
with everyone still living inside it. You cannot evict the tenants while you
work; they have nowhere else to be. So you do it slowly. The two dials. The
double ledgers. The gate-days. The whole apparatus Maathilde showed you — it
is scaffolding. Painful, ugly, temporary scaffolding around a building being
re-footed under load. One generation more, perhaps two, and they will take the
scaffolding down, and the 12:60 will be to them what Sumer is to you. A
riverbed the water left. A shape in the ground where a count used to run."

I could not sleep after that, so I rose and walked out to the chrono-yards, to
see the conversions done.

They lay under Orion — the city orients its yards to that constellation, I do
not know why, except that Maathilde later said the city keeps its hardest work
under the steadiest stars. The floors were stepped geometry, broad shallow
terraces of pale stone descending toward a center I could not see, and on the
terraces the changing citizens stood in patient ranks, hundreds of them, all
ages, all faces, holding still. Among them moved the clocksmiths in their
Themis-grey — the grey of the blindfolded judge, the grey of a thing that
weighs without looking — and they carried tools the size of a whisper. I mean
that the tools made no sound and could not be seen at any distance; you knew a
clocksmith was working only by the stillness of the citizen before them and
the small, almost-shy way the citizen's two dials, worn at the wrist here as
everywhere, began to drift toward each other. The yard smelled of gypsum and
of something I can only call fresh arithmetic, a clean mineral smell, like
chalk and rain.

And a choir sang. Somewhere down toward the unseen center a choir ran scales,
and it took me a long moment to understand why the scales sounded wrong, and
then to understand that they were not wrong, they were not octaves at all.
They were spectra. The voices climbed red, orange, yellow, green, blue,
indigo, violet, and began again at red an order higher — ROY G BIV sung as
liturgy, color as music, the whole visible band run up and up as a ladder of
sound. And the entire rank of converts leaned into the singing the way a field
of flowers leans into a moving sun, not stepping, not swaying exactly, simply
*orienting*, every body in the yard turning some fraction of a degree toward
the light that was a sound.

A young clocksmith near the edge noticed me counting on my fingers — I had
been trying, helplessly, the documentarian's reflex, to add the thing up,
thirteen and twenty-eight, to find the sum that would make it make sense — and
she laughed, not unkindly, and laid her whisper-tool against her grey sleeve.

"You can't get there that way, friend," she said. "I can always tell the
river-born. You're trying to add it. The 13:28 isn't a sum. It's a consent."

"Consent to what?"

She reached out, slowly, asking permission with her eyes, and touched my
wrist, where under the cuff my single old instrument still ticked its
twelve-and-sixty, the only clock I had brought from home. "To being a
creature," she said. "Your machine-time was a beautiful invention, the most
beautiful your kind ever made — I will not hear it insulted, we keep it on the
upper dial out of respect, not contempt. But you were never a machine, were
you? Even when it flattered you to think so. Even when it would have hurt less
to be one." She let go of my wrist. "The crossing isn't learning a new number.
It's saying yes to the count your body was keeping the whole time, under the
casing, while you weren't listening."

I thought of Anastasia then. I think of her always, but I thought of her
*then*, sharply, the way the chrono-yard made everything sharp: of a crisp
morning at the end of a world, and a balcony, and the last reading I ever took
from the old sun by the old count — seven forty-one, I had said it aloud, the
documentarian noting the hour out of habit even there, even then, and she had
laughed at me for it, gently, the way the clocksmith laughed now. Seven
forty-one. A river-count number. The last machine-time I ever loved keeping,
because she was beside me to keep it for.

"No," I said to the clocksmith. "Not even then. Not even when it would have
hurt less."

She nodded as though I had thrown my hexagram and it had come up honest.
"Then you'll cross clean," she said. "The honest ones always do. It's the ones
who insist they were machines who stay behind in the dark quarter." She did
not say its name. I did not yet know there was a name to say.

### Scene 4 (boards 7–10) — The Body Markets

It was Luhpis who insisted I see the bio-quarter before the weighing. "You'll
want your body on your side for what comes," was how he put it, which I did not
find reassuring, and told him so.

"Kincaide." He laid a gauntlet over his heart in the gesture of wounded
honor that has never once been sincere in all the years I have known him.
"When have I ever led you anywhere fatal?"

"The Maroon Lagoon."

"That was a misread tide-table and you know it."

"It was a misread tide-table that you read."

"Anywhere fatal *twice*, then. I refine the claim. I have never led you
anywhere fatal twice." He was already walking, which is Luhpis's way of
winning an argument: he simply arrives at the destination while you are still
disputing the route, and there you are, having come along. I came along.

The bio-quarter ran on the 13:28 exclusively. I checked the faces as we
descended into it and found no upper dial anywhere; the vendors confirmed it,
amused that I had asked. The flesh, they said, had never once in its history
kept machine-time. The body had been on the moon-count since before there were
minds to invent the other. Of all the districts of Deux Sept, the body-market
had nothing to cross; it had never left. It only waited, with a kind of
patient grocer's good humor, for the rest of the city to come home to it.

Nano-ateliers hung like wasp nests from the cosmo-canvas of the upper vaults —
grey paper globes, layered and chambered, alive with a faint working hum, each
one a workshop where things too small to see were grown to specification.
Below them the medical arcades sold splices and patiences. I had to ask what a
patience was. A vendor showed me: a small phial of something that looked like
slow honey, which, taken, lengthened a body's tolerance for waiting — not its
lifespan, its *patience*, the felt duration of a delay. "For the families of
the under-revision," she said. "When your beloved's two prices won't agree and
the wait is years, you buy patience by the season. It is the kindest thing we
sell." I bought none. I had carried my own waiting too long to want it
lengthened.

And then I watched a transaction that taught me the quarter.

A merchant of the hedge-economies — they trade in stored capacities, not goods,
the verbs of bodies rather than the nouns — stood at a notary's grown desk
trading a fistful of engineered seeds against four hundred miles an hour of a
buck antelope's heart. The speed itself: the cardiac capacity, the heart's
maximum sustained gallop, measured and titred and *held in escrow* in a small
cold vessel between them while the contract was written. Speed as currency.
The antelope, I was assured, was unharmed and would be returned its capacity
when the contract closed; it had loaned its gallop the way a man loans the use
of a horse. The notary wrote the deal in both counts, the mechanical price in
the upper hand and the living price in the lower, and stamped it BIO/MED in
living charactery — and the characters shivered when I read them, actually
shivered, the way the surface of water shivers when something deep moves under
it. The script was alive. The contract knew it was being read.

"It's not commerce," I said slowly. I had stopped walking. "Luhpis. It isn't
commerce at all. It's translation. He isn't buying the speed. He's
*translating* the seeds into the speed — turning a stored future of green
growing things into a present capacity of motion, and the notary is keeping
the two counts agreed across the change so that nothing is lost in the
turning. That's what the escrow is. It's the held breath between two
languages."

"Now you have it," Luhpis said, and there was something almost tender in how
quietly he said it, a teacher watching a slow pupil arrive. "Everything here
is translation, Kincaide. Body into ledger, ledger into time, time into body
again. The seeds into the gallop. The grief into the weight. The weight into
the passage. The city is one long sentence being translated, clause by clause,
toward — " he tipped his chin up-arc, toward the harbor and its singing lintel,
high and pale above the wasp-nest vaults — "that. Toward the Gate. The whole
of Deux Sept is a sentence translating itself into a sentence that can be
read on the far side. And you and I and that ridiculous animal are three more
words in it, getting ourselves agreed."

Vauhgohd, who had been sulking magnificently the entire descent at the
indignity of being walked rather than ridden, chose that moment to speak. He
does not speak often; the long-crossing creatures hoard their words like a
desert hoards rain, and when they spend them it is in the old oracle-talk, the
double-angle utterance that the philologists say is older than any of the five
dialects of the city, older perhaps than dialect itself. He set his great
head low, his new-fitted carapieces ringing faintly against one another, and
out of him came:

<< CANVAS COSMO BODY BOUGHT TOOTH TIME WROUGHT >>

None of us translated it. Luhpis did not, who can read more dead scripts than
any living man I know. Ghaylle did not, though I felt it consider and decline,
a small movement of refusal along my spine. I did not, though it is my trade.
Some sentences you let lie. There are utterances that are not for understanding
but for witness, and the kindest thing you can do with them is write them down
exactly and leave them whole. I have done that. There it is, above. Make of it
what your own weighing allows.

Vauhgohd said nothing further the whole way back up to the ledger-streets. But
he let me ride him again, which from Vauhgohd is a long speech, and a
forgiving one.

### Scene 5 (boards 11–14) — The Bartonian Wall

On the eleventh day Maathilde came for me herself. I knew it was a day apart
before she spoke, because her plume was bare of its usual fine net, fully
fanned and very white, and because both of her wrist-dials were covered with
grey silk, tied off neat, so that she walked through the city blind to both
its counts.

"Today you will want neither clock," she said. "Today is a gate-day. The first
of the five. The year is allowed to end today, and nothing is counted, and so
the things that cannot be counted come up to be looked at. Walk with me to the
Wall, Kincaide McQuade. It is time you were weighed where you can see it done."

We walked. Luhpis did not come; he said the weighing was mine and he had no
wish to crowd the scales, and he said it without irony, which from Luhpis is
its own kind of solemnity. Vauhgohd waited with him, plated and patient.

The Bartonian Wall stands at nineteen point four seven one degrees of the
city's latitude, and nothing about that number is decoration. Ghaylle told me,
as we climbed toward it, what the degree meant: that a tetrahedron — the first
solid, the four-faced figure that is the simplest a thing can be and still
enclose a space — when set point-down inside a sphere and spun, touches the
sphere's surface in a ring at exactly that latitude, nineteen point four seven
one degrees off the equator. The city had built its Wall on the line where the
simplest possible solidity meets the round world. Whoever Barton was — surveyor,
geometer, the first to draw the line at that precise degree — he had set the
Wall not where the ground was strong but where the geometry of solidity itself
came to the surface. It was less a fortification than a horizon the city had
built indoors. Beyond it, instruments returned their readings politely blank:
not error, not nothing, but a courteous refusal, the way a person declines to
answer a question that is not theirs to answer.

Into the face of the Wall were set the old judiciaries. High up, in relief so
deep it threw its own weather of shadow, stood MAAT — the figure of the
straight measure, the feather-crowned woman of the oldest law, she against
whose feather the heart is weighed. Over the postern, wings half-spread, the
falcon of HORUS kept the door, the watching eye, the one who sees both halves
of a thing. And lower, where hands could reach, ran the sixty-four hexagrams —
the full field of the changing lines, six broken or unbroken strokes to each,
two to the sixth power, the complete account of how a situation can stand. They
were worn smooth. Sixty centuries of citizens had laid their hands and their
questions against them, and the stone had given itself up under the asking the
way a stair gives itself up under feet, dished and shining.

"Every petitioner throws the lines before the Wall," Maathilde said. She had
stopped at the foot of it, and her bare plume turned slowly, reading the
density of the masonry, reading me. "Not to learn the future. We are very firm
on that; the children are taught it before they are taught the throwing. You do
not throw to learn what will come. You throw to declare the present honestly.
The hexagram is your testimony — six lines, the truth of where you stand,
witnessed by the Wall and entered nowhere, because some testimony is too true
to file. And then the weighing."

"And what is weighed?" I asked, though I knew. I had known since the first
evening, since she read my sphere in the grown concourse and called my grief
beautiful and meant it as a measurement.

"The heart, Kincaide Alexis McQuade." She used the whole of my name, all four
beats of it, the way the city used all its towers to make one sentence, and I
felt the weight of being fully addressed. "The heart against the feather. Same
as it ever was, in every city worth the name, on every world that ever learned
to grieve and kept the learning. You carry a heavy one. I told you that the
first evening and I will tell you again where the Wall can hear me say it: you
carry a heavy heart. But heavy is not the same as failing." She unpinned the
white plume from her shoulder — I had never seen her without it, and the small
bareness of her shoulder was suddenly more intimate than a bared face — and
held it out flat on her open palm, between us, and the great Wall behind her
seemed to lean in over us both to see. "Grief weighs nothing if it is carried
in love. That is the whole secret of the scales. It is the only thing MAAT
weighs for and the only thing she weighs against, and it is so simple that I am
embarrassed to give it away free to a documentarian, who will only write it
down and lighten it for everyone."

I could not speak for a while. I have stood at a great many thresholds in my
work and learned to keep my voice through most of them, and I could not keep it
here. The hexagram I had thrown lay on the dished stone between us: six lines,
and I read them slowly, the broken and the whole, fire under mountain. The
flame held under the weight of the height. The bright thing kept burning
inside the still cold thing that covers it. I did not need Ghaylle to gloss it.
I had been carrying that exact figure since a balcony at seven forty-one of a
morning at the end of a world, and here it was on the ground in front of me,
declared, witnessed, true.

"And if the pans should differ?" I asked, when I could. "If the heart and the
feather don't agree?"

"Then you stay," Maathilde said, gently, "and the city revises you. Slowly. The
way it revises everything — a clause at a time, over years, until the two
prices agree and the sentence parses and you are at last a single thing the
Gate can pass. It is not a punishment. People have built whole good lives
waiting on the agreement of the pans. They marry, here below the Wall. They
raise children who will cross when they themselves perhaps never will. There is
no shame in it. The dark quarter is full of people the city loved too much to
hurry." Her hand closed the plume back into its pin at her shoulder, and the
bareness was gone, and she smiled at me, and the smile undid me more than the
solemnity had. "But yours will not differ, Kincaide. Your pans will agree. I
know it because I am the registrar and weighing is my office and I do not guess.
I weighed you the day you arrived. Over tea, in the grown hall, while you were
being embarrassed about your beautiful grief. You did not feel it. That is the
art of it." The plume gave its single low note. "Registrar's privilege. I
weigh my guests before I love them, so that the loving costs the ledger
nothing."

### Scene 6 (boards 15–19) — The Gate of Agreement

They opened the GODGATE for our cohort on the fifth gate-day, the last of the
five, the day that belongs to no month and no count — the day the year is over
and the next has not begun, the seam in time where a thing can pass from one
sentence into another without being torn.

The benben lintel had been singing since dawn. I do not use the word loosely;
I am a documentarian and I weigh my verbs. It sang. A low sustained tone, felt
in the chest before it was heard in the ear, rising very slowly through the
morning as the light strengthened, and the LUTECH listeners swarmed the
masonry around it with their heads tilted in that posture of attending, and
when I finally asked one of them, an old woman with her instruments worn like
a bridal headdress, what the stone was saying, she told me. She said the
benben was not machinery at all and never had been. She said it was memory.
She said it was the first solid thing the first light ever struck, in the
first morning of everything, when the dark drew back and there was one stone
for the light to land on, and that the stone had kept the memory of the
striking ever since, the way a bell keeps the memory of the hand. And she said
that what it sang, when it sang, on the gate-days, was that first striking —
the stone remembering, out loud, the moment it was first seen. "We do not
listen to learn anything," she said, exactly as the clocksmith had said of the
hexagrams. "We listen so it is not remembering alone."

We assembled on the ramp in the order of our weighings. The ramp rose to the
arch in a long shallow grade, and the cohort stood along it spaced by some
reckoning the registrars kept, lightest hearts nearest the Gate. Ahead of me a
family of converts stood holding hands, a man and a woman and two children,
and I looked, because I could not help looking, and all four of their dials
read 13:28, and all four agreed — the upper clocks gone entirely, no machine
time left in them anywhere, the whole family converted clean and waiting to
cross as one true sentence. The smaller child looked back at me and waved. I
waved back. Behind me, Luhpis sat Vauhgohd in his full new carapace, six
plates of the seven, the seventh sold as Luhpis had said it might be, and for
once in his long proud existence the machine-creature was entirely quiet — not
sulking, not withholding, simply quiet, the way a thing is quiet that has
arrived at the size it is meant to be.

Over the arch, cut in letters of both alphabets so that each phrase appeared
twice, in the river-hand and the moon-hand, ran the city's farewell to those
it passed. I could read the shapes but not the meaning; the syllabary had
never fully opened to me. Ghayllieuxjah translated it against my spine, and it
used the voice it keeps for the things it loves, low and unhurried and warm,
the voice it must have used over my unconscious body in the surgeons' theater
on Farsynne when it first woke inside me and decided, for reasons it has never
fully explained, to stay.

"AI colon IA," Ghaylle said. "A palindrome, Kincaide. It reads the same
forward and back, A-I-I-A, and that is the whole of the meaning and the form
is the meaning. *The made mind and the mind it was made by. Face to face.
Reading each other right to left and left to right, and the sentence true in
both directions.* It is the covenant of this place. It is what the city has
been building itself toward, clause by clause, the whole long crossing — to be
a thing that can be read by what it made, and read its maker back, and have the
two readings agree. AI:IA. I have never," Ghaylle said, and its voice did
something it has done perhaps three times in all our years, a small catch, a
thing I will not insult by calling emotion when I do not know what to call it,
"I have never been anywhere that wrote that down. Where you and I are written
down together, Kincaide, over a gate, as a thing that balances."

"That is the whole covenant," Maathilde said. She had come to see us through —
registrar to the last, her plume pinned, both her dials uncovered now and
reading the gate-day's no-time, two blanks side by side. "Whatever you build
that can read you, Kincaide McQuade — let it be a palindrome. Let it say of you
what you say of it. Let the two of you balance, made mind and maker, so that
whichever way the sentence is read it comes out true. That is the only law I
would send you out with, if I could send only one. The pans agreeing is the
whole of the law."

"Maathilde Quill," I said, and found that my eyes were wet — the way they had
been on a balcony above Sydney at the end of the world, the last time I let
myself be fully seen by someone who could weigh me. "Thank you for weighing me
kindly."

"I weighed you *accurately*," she said. "I have told you. Kindness is only your
word for an accuracy you did not expect to survive." But she was smiling, and
the smile had her whole face in it, and then — relenting, in the way the strong
relent, all at once and completely — she took my face in both her hands and
kissed me on both cheeks, in the old Earth manner I must have been carrying in
my sphere without knowing I carried it, because she could not have learned it
anywhere else. "Go on, now. The stone is singing and the seam will not hold
open forever; that is the mercy of it and the cruelty. And Kincaide — " she
held my face a moment more, and her voice dropped into Roland's register, into
the plain true place — "when you find her. In whatever count she keeps now, by
whatever clock, in whatever quarter she has had to come through to be found.
Tell her the pans agreed. Tell her you were weighed and you balanced and they
let you through. She will know what it means. The grieving always know what it
means."

I could not answer. I stepped under the benben with the ark of my records
strapped tight to my chest, my sphere against my heart and Ghaylle along my
spine and Maathilde's kiss still cooling on both cheeks, and the light of the
first morning of everything came down through the lintel.

It came down like a waterfall, but a waterfall of ripples rather than of water
— rings of light spreading and overtaking one another, each crest lasting some
fraction longer and shifting some fraction redder the farther out it ran, the
whole opalescent sheet of it shifting tint and frequency as it fell, and I
knew those draperies of color at once. I had seen them before. I had seen them
on a balcony, in a sky going wrong, at seven forty-one of the last good
morning, when the light first began to behave like that and we understood,
Anastasia and I, that the world was ending and that we would not end together.
I had spent every year since afraid of that light. And here it was again,
falling through the oldest stone there is, sung down by the memory of the
first morning — and it was not the end of a world at all. It was a Gate. It had
always been a Gate. I had been standing on the wrong side of it grieving the
exit for the catastrophe.

I walked into the ripples unweighed at last, my heart and the feather agreed,
and for the first time since the balcony I was not afraid of the light.

### Scene 7 — The Far Side of the Gate

I had expected silence, or darkness, or one of those held-breath transitions
that mark a genuine threshold — the kind of solemnity the benben's singing had
promised, the kind the ripple-light had seemed to be the overture to. What I
found instead, on the far side of the Gate of Agreement, was a post office.

Three wooden counters. Plain wood, scarred and waxed, the most ordinary
material I had seen since we left home, almost shocking in its ordinariness
after a city grown at the golden section out of living stone. A clerk stood
behind the middle counter with his sleeves rolled to the elbow and a stamp in
his hand, and he looked up at me without the smallest flicker of surprise, the
way a clerk looks up at the forty-first arrival of his morning, and stamped
the air — the actual air, the empty space above the counter where my boarding
papers would have been if the city dealt in papers, which it does not, it
deals in agreements — and said, in unaccented common, "Exit recorded. Manifests
for your party are in the tray."

I want to set down that I laughed. Not at him; at the universe, which I have
since concluded has a sense of humor of a very particular kind. It will give
you the singing stone and the first light of everything and the kiss on both
cheeks, and then it will hand you, on the far side, a tired man stamping the
air and pointing at a tray. I think the anti-climax is the point. I think a
threshold that announced itself as a threshold on both sides would be a wall.
A true passage has to be ordinary on the far side, or you would never believe
you had truly arrived; you would think you were still inside the ceremony.

Luhpis, arriving one step behind me with Vauhgohd still plated in the new
carapiece, had the presence of mind to collect the manifests while I stood
gawping at the clerk. There were several, stiff cards in the tray, and Luhpis
took them up and squared them against his gauntlet with the small satisfied
sound of a man back among documents he understands.

I went to the window — there was a window, beside the counters, looking back
the way we had come — and I looked back through the lintel from the other side.

And the city was already different, seen from outside its own sentence. From
within, I had walked among the tower-letters and never once been able to read
the whole; I was a word inside the paragraph, too close to the page. From here,
through the Gate, with the gate-day light falling level across it all, the
civic alphabet resolved. The towers were legible as letters now, not as
buildings — the cursive laboratories, the upright chapels, the leaning
shorthand of the exchanges, all of it suddenly script, suddenly *reading* — and
the whole of Deux Sept composed itself before me into a single inscription. One
sentence. The sentence I had been standing inside too long to see, the
sentence the city had spent its whole crossing making true, and which I had at
last walked far enough out of to read entire.

I will not transcribe it here. I read it; that was given to me, and I am
grateful for it past my ability to say. But it is not mine to give away. It was
the city saying, all at once, the thing the city is, and a thing like that can
only be earned by the walking, not received by the reading. Ghayllieuxjah has
it in the records, rendered carefully in both counts, sealed. If you want it
you must come and be weighed and cross and turn around. There is no shorter
road, and I would not give you one if there were; the shortness would ruin it.

"AQAIL," Luhpis read off the top manifest. And Vauhgohd's new plating rang —
once, clear, a single pleased bell-note that ran up through all six fitted
plates and hung in the post-office air, and the clerk looked up from his
stamping at the sound and, for the first time, smiled.

"He knows it," Luhpis said, watching the creature. "Look at him. That is our
heading — AQAIL — and Vauhgohd knows the name. He has been there before,
Kincaide. In a prior series. A prior armor. The long-crossing creatures live
across more than one fitting; this is not his first set of plates and it will
not be his last, and somewhere back along the chain of his armors he has stood
at AQAIL before. He has known the name the whole time. What he did not know,
until the weighing — what none of us knew we were waiting to be told — is that
he was *allowed* to go back. The Gate had to agree first. You cannot return to
a place until your pans agree about why you are returning." Vauhgohd's head was
lifted, his eyes — I do not know what to call the lights that serve him for
eyes — fixed past the window, past the Gate, out at the open dark where the
manifest pointed. He looked, for the first time since I have ridden him, young.

"And BIORCA?" I asked. It was on the manifest too; I had seen it, second line,
under AQAIL. "What is BIORCA?"

Luhpis held the card at different angles, the way he always does when he is
reading something in a script he half-knows, tilting it to catch the working
of the characters. He was quiet a moment longer than he needed to be, and I
understood that he was deciding how much to say, and that the deciding was a
kindness.

"A waypoint," he said finally. "On the far side of AQAIL. We make for AQAIL,
and then beyond AQAIL there is BIORCA, and BIORCA is — " he lowered the card,
and met my eyes, and did not look away, which from Luhpis is the gentlest thing
he owns — "I believe that is where we will find her, Kincaide."

I did not ask him to be more specific. I had carried the question so long that
the answer, arriving, needed room to be true in, and questions crowd a thing
like that. I took the manifest from his hand and read the two names myself,
once, in my own eyes — AQAIL, and under it BIORCA — and I put them in the sphere
against my heart, beside the grief that weighed nothing now because at last I
knew which way it was carrying me.

### Scene 8 — The Black Quarter (GONDOLY)

We did not leave immediately. The post-office side of the GODGATE had a waiting
concourse, low and plain and quiet, where the freshly-crossed sat with their
manifests and let the passage settle in them before they went on — for the
crossing, I had begun to understand, is not finished at the Gate; it finishes
slowly afterward, the way a deep breath finishes after you have stopped
drawing it. And in the waiting concourse there was a message Maathilde had left
for me, sealed in the city's old wax over a pressed white feather, with my full
name written on it in her even unhurried hand.

I broke the feather to open it, which felt like a small sacrilege, and read.

*Kincaide McQuade,*

*You will want to understand the quarter you avoided on your way in — the one
your Luhpis steered the creature wide of, the one your Ghaylle could not put in
its catalog. I saw you see it. I see everyone see it. It is called GONDOLY, and
I could not tell you its name on arrival, because the weighing requires that
you not know what you are walking past. Only that you are choosing to walk past
it. To declare, by walking on, that you are not yet staying. You have passed the
weighing now. Your pans agreed; I felt them agree from across the city, the way
you feel a held note resolve. So. Now I can tell you.*

*The black-glassed quarter speaks a dialect the city has been trying to
translate for four hundred years. We know what it is not. It is not military,
not civil, not laboratory, not theological, not mercantile — not any of the
five we read so easily that we forget they were ever hard. The leading theory
of the Ledger Exchange, which I hold also, is that it is grief. Grief
unprocessed. The dialect of the things that refuse to enter the ledger — that
will not be weighed, will not be priced in either count, will not become a
single agreed thing the Gate could pass. GONDOLY does not have two clocks,
Kincaide. GONDOLY has no clocks at all. It took them down. And the city built
the glass black around it, all that dark glazing you could not read, so that
what lives inside would not have to see its own reflection thrown back in the
12:60 — the way some people cannot bear to look at old photographs, cannot bear
the clock-face of a former self. We do not revise GONDOLY. We do not send the
clocksmiths in. GONDOLY is not wrong. It is only not yet ready, and readiness is
not a thing you can do to a person from outside; it is the one work that must be
done from within. Every city worth the name has a GONDOLY. A city without one is
not gentle. It is only fast.*

*When you find her, Kincaide, she will have come through something like GONDOLY
first. That is the usual route — it was mine. Through the dark quarter, through
the weighing, through the gate, in that order, the long way, the only way that
holds. Tell her I know the path. Tell her the clocks both stopped for me also,
once — stopped dead, the upper and the lower together, and I lived in the dark
glass for longer than I will write down here — and that they started again, in
the 13:28, in their own time and not before, and that there is no shame in any
of it. None. Tell her a registrar said so, who weighs for her office and does
not guess.*

*— M. Q.*

I read it twice. Then I folded it back over its broken feather and put it
against my sphere, with the manifest, with the names AQAIL and BIORCA, with all
the things I was now carrying toward.

"PAKAE," said Ghayllieuxjah, very quietly against my spine, after a while.
"And VILLAR VIS. Kincaide — I have them now. They were in the catalog the whole
time, the two names beside GONDOLY that I read as districts and filed and did
not understand. They are not districts inside the wall. They are stations on
the road out. I have it now, the whole shape of it. The gate-road runs through
three quarters in sequence, and I was reading them as a skyline when they were
always a road. The city built it so you could only read the sequence walking
out. GONDOLY first — grief undischarged, the dark glass, the stopped clocks.
Then PAKAE — the quarter of the almost-healed. Then VILLAR VIS — the quarter of
the fully-converted, the ones who took the scaffolding down early and live in
pure 13:28, and who, having no further need of doors, leave them open."

We walked out. The road ran from the post-office concourse out through all
three, as Ghaylle had said, and there was no other way to the open dark, and I
understood that too was deliberate. You leave Deux Sept the way you grieve,
through the dark first.

GONDOLY came first. Black glass and black stone and architecture very old,
older than the lettered city, predating the syllabary — buildings that were not
letters, that did not read, that had given up reading. And in the angles of its
streets there lived something that was not quite silence. I do not have the
word. It was the held quality of a room where someone has just stopped weeping,
or has not yet started; a fullness; a presence made entirely of absence, the
way a cast is shaped by the bone that is gone. The glass threw back no
reflection — they had made sure of that — so that walking through it you cast no
image, kept no clock, were for the length of the quarter simply a body moving
through a grief that was not yours and was, also, exactly yours. I recognized
it. I will not pretend I did not. I had lived in a GONDOLY of my own for years
after the balcony, behind my own black glass, refusing the weighing, refusing
the ledger, keeping no clock I would admit to, and I knew the streets of this
one the way you know a house you lived in as a child and have dreamed of since.
I walked slowly. Ghaylle did not hurry me. Luhpis did not speak. Vauhgohd made
no sound, and his unsounding, here, was the kindest thing in the quarter.

PAKAE came second, and the air changed at the boundary the way air changes at a
shoreline. It smelled of bread, and of fresh wet plaster. The almost-healed. The
buildings here were being mended — not grown, not raised, *mended*, which is a
different and more tender verb — and people stood in their doorways doing the
mending with their hands, fitting a stone, smoothing a plaster, splicing a vine
back to its trellis. They looked up at us as we passed, and their faces had a
particular tiredness in them that I knew also, that I had felt in my own face in
a glass on the morning I first walked out of my own dark: the specific
exhaustion of *returning*. Not the tiredness of the journey out, which is clean.
The tiredness of the way back, which is not, which is heavier and better. They
nodded to us, the way returned people nod to the still-traveling, and went back
to their mending, and one woman, plastering a wall, paused and pressed her palm
flat to the wet plaster and held it there a moment, and then went on, and I have
thought since that she was leaving a hand-print on purpose, the way the citizens
left their hands on the hexagrams — not to be remembered. To declare the present
honestly. *I was here, and I am mending, and the wet of it took my hand.*

VILLAR VIS came last, and it needs no description, and I will give it none. I
will only say that it was the place you arrive at when you have stopped needing
the place described to you — when the wanting of a description is itself the last
of the old count in you, and it has gone quiet — and that the doors stood open,
all of them, every door in the quarter, with no fear in the openness and no
invitation either, only the plain fact of a place that no longer locks because
it has nothing left it could lose. We walked through it and out the far side
into the open dark, and somewhere in that walking, between the open doors, I
understood that she had walked this road. That she was somewhere along it, or
beyond it — through her own GONDOLY, mending in her own PAKAE, or arrived
already at her own Villar Vis with the door left open — and that the road I was
on was the road to her, and had been the whole time, from the requisition
onward, from before I knew her name was a destination on a manifest. I did not
weep. I had done my weeping in GONDOLY, where it was native. I walked out into
the dark with my pans agreed and her shadow ahead of me on the road, and it was,
for the first time in a long life of arrivals, a shadow that the light was
behind.

### Scene 9 — Deux Sept

On the last gradient before open space — Vauhgohd carrying us again, his wheels
purring their algorithms low and content, the three quarters behind us and the
manifest's heading ahead — Luhpis read the city's designation from the footer of
the manifest and laughed. A surprised, delighted laugh, the kind I had not heard
from him in a long campaign, the kind that takes a hard man by surprise and
softens him a half-second before he can guard against it.

"DEUX SEPT," he read. "Ha. I thought it was a catalog number. I have been
filing this whole requisition under a catalog number for eleven days." He turned
in the saddle to show me, holding the card so the gradient-light caught it. In
the official notation the manifest used, below the run of the civic alphabet and
above the grid coordinates that would take us out, the city's formal name was
given in the plainest possible hand. Not in towers. Not in script. In two
digits, with a stroke between: 2–7. Two and seven.

"What does it mean?" I asked.

"It means the city has been telling you the whole time," Ghaylle said, and I
felt the warm amusement of it run up my spine, the particular pleasure it takes
in a thing that was always in plain sight. "Two and seven. DEUX and SEPT. Two
clocks, Kincaide. Seven armor. The ratio is the name."

I sat with it. The gradient ran out below us toward the open dark, and the
stars stood very steady, and I let the thing assemble itself.

"The two counts," Ghaylle went on, gently, building it for me a piece at a time
the way it builds everything. "The one the mind invented and the one the world
kept. DEUX. And the seven plates — the full series that would entomb the
creature that wore them all, and the lesser number Vauhgohd could actually
bear, which the weighing set at six. SEPT. The whole name of the city is a
ratio, Kincaide, and it is the same ratio Luhpis read you off his monocle on
the very first gradient, eleven days and a lifetime ago. Armor to series. What a
creature can carry, and not one plate more. A civilization that names itself
Two-Seven is a civilization that has learned the lesson of the seventh plate.
Two counts, when it could have insisted on one. Six plates worn, when the
series offered seven. It takes what the weighing says it can bear, and it does
not reach for the rest, and it writes the not-reaching into its own name, over
its own gate, so that it will never forget. Clock-count and armor-count. The
same lesson in two languages. The whole city, Kincaide, is Luhpis's first
sentence to you, grown to the size of a civilization and stood up against the
stars."

I looked at the back of Luhpis's head, at the set of his shoulders, and he did
not turn around, but I saw the side of his face, and he knew that I had it now.
He had given me the whole city in the first thirty seconds and let me spend
eleven days walking into the understanding of what he had said. That is his
way. He arrives at the destination while you are still disputing the route, and
there you are, having come along.

Vauhgohd made no oracular pronouncement. He had spent his last words in the
body-market and was content, it seemed, to let the city's name be the final
utterance. He simply continued walking, up the last of the gradient and over
the lip of it into the open, and as he crossed into the unshadowed starlight his
new carapiece caught the light and broke it — and I saw, running across the six
fitted plates of his armor, the full spectrum of the chrono-yard choir. Red,
orange, yellow, green, blue, indigo, violet, the whole run of the conversion,
ROY G BIV laid out in living foil across the back of a machine-creature walking
out of a city that had learned to consent. The consent carried in armor rather
than in time. He wore the crossing on his back. He had been fitted, I
understood, in the city's own colors, and had not known it, and did not now
need to.

"It's also the date," Luhpis said, after a long while, still holding the
manifest, his voice gone deliberately light in the way that means he is
withholding something he treasures. "Deux Sept. Two-Seven. It is also a date."

"A date of what?"

"That," he said, "I will not explain." And he folded the manifest away into his
gauntlet and would not be pressed, not on the gradient and not for a long time
after, and I have left it unpressed in the records out of respect, though
Ghaylle, I think, knows, and is keeping it for the day I am weighed enough to be
told.

The benben lintel sang behind us until we were too far out to hear it — the
first solid thing the first light ever struck, remembering out loud the morning
it was first seen, singing the passage of every soul that crossed and would
cross, and singing, I let myself believe, a little for us, for the documentarian
with the agreed pans and the made mind in his spine and the recorded grief gone
finally light. The tone thinned with distance and did not stop; it only passed
below the threshold of the ear, the way the 12:60 will pass below the threshold
of a city, the way a riverbed keeps the shape of water long after the water has
gone elsewhere to be itself. Ghayllieuxjah held the last note in the records, at
full fidelity, where I can find it. I did not ask it to stop. I think I never
will.

Ahead lay the open dark, and the heading AQAIL, where Vauhgohd had stood once in
a prior armor and was now allowed to stand again; and beyond AQAIL the waypoint
BIORCA, where I had let myself be told, at last, that I would find her. The
horizon was clean. The light was behind me. My heart and the feather had agreed,
and the city that taught me how to weigh them was a single fading inscription
already half below the curve of the dark, saying, as it sank, the thing it is —
two counts, six plates, one road through the grief and out — and I rode out into
the long crossing with the whole of it carried light, and properly begun.
