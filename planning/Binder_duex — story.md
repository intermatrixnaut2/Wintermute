# Deux Sept: The City of Two Clocks

*First-round fleshing of `Binder_duex.pdf` (boards 1–19), narrative register
(Viaticum). Developed from the symbol streams of the boards; the drawings
themselves were not viewable in this remote session, so every scene below is
a first interpretation meant to be corrected against the artwork.*

## Voice profile

First-person past tense, Kincaide McQuade narrating. Sentences conventional
and earnest; the strangeness lives in what they describe, never in how.
Material detail does the world-building — minerals, foils, veined and living
machinery. Dialogue carries the feeling: characters reassure, plead, tease,
and bless each other, full names spoken at emotional peaks, farewells staged
as set-pieces. Invented words appear rarely and land hard.

## Scenes

### Scene 1 (boards 1–2) — Arrival at the Astrocity

The descent into RMR was nothing like the brochures of memory I had carried
from Earth. Vauhgohd took the last gradient at a canter, wheels purring their
algorithms, and the Astrocity rose out of the stellar shallows like a reef
that had decided, long ago, to mean something. Every tower was lettered. I do
not mean signage. I mean the towers themselves were letters, a civic alphabet
laid out in strata — the laboratories cursive, the chapels upright, the
merchant exchanges a quick shorthand leaning into the wind of orbit.

"Ora Aura," Luhpis said, by way of introduction, sweeping a gauntleted hand
across the skyline. "The aura is the ora. The city prays what it is."

"It's a syllabary," I said. "The whole city is a sentence."

"A sentence still being revised. Mind the grammar, Kincaide. Some districts
bite." He smiled when he said it, but I noticed he steered Vauhgohd wide of a
black-glassed quarter whose letterforms I could not read at all.

Ghayllieuxjah spoke warm against my spine. "I have begun the catalog. Civic
dialects detected: military, civil, laboratory, theological, mercantile.
Also one dialect that does not answer to any of these. I will flag it."

"Flag it gently," I said. "We are guests."

Above the harbor gates — and I will be careful here, because I did not
believe it myself — stood an arch the locals wrote as GODGATE, a lintel of
benben-stone older than the city it admitted. Lunar foundries ringed it,
LUTECH crews swarming the masonry with tools of light, not repairing it,
I would learn, but *listening* to it.

### Scene 2 (boards 3–4) — The Ledger Quarter

They billeted us above the Ledger Quarter, where the city kept its two
clocks. I noticed them the first evening: every public face bore two dials,
one reading 12:60, one reading 13:28, and no two citizens consulted the same
one.

Our hostess in that quarter was a registrar of the theological exchange. She
received us beneath a concourse Gaudí would have wept inside — columns grown,
not raised, every capital budding off at the golden section, .618 of the way
to heaven and then flowering. She was new to me, so I set her name down
carefully: Maathilde Quill, and on her shoulder she wore a single white
plume, and I understood without being told that it was not decoration but
instrumentation.

"You are the Farsynne documentarian," she said. "Kincaide Alexis McQuade.
We have read your sphere. You grieve beautifully, if you will permit me."

"I — thank you. I think." Luhpis coughed into his glove.

"Do not be embarrassed. Grief is a unit of account here. We weigh it." She
touched the plume. "Everything entered in the city's books — every cargo,
every covenant, every art object out of the xeno-ateliers — is weighed once
in the 12:60 and once in the 13:28. The mechanical price and the living
price. The ledgers must agree before the Gate will pass anything through."

"And when they don't agree?"

Her voice went quiet the way Roland's used to. "Then the city revises its
sentence."

### Scene 3 (boards 5–6) — The Two Clocks

I asked Ghayllieuxjah, on the third night, which clock we kept.

"You were born into the 12:60," Ghaylle said. "Sixty minutes, twelve months,
the machine count. The 13:28 is the moon count — thirteen turnings,
twenty-eight days. The city runs both because its citizens arrive from both.
Conversion is the chief local industry, and the chief local injury."

"Injury?"

"Time is load-bearing, Kincaide. Change the count and the building of you
groans."

I walked out to the chrono-yards to see the conversions done. Under Orion,
on floors of stepped geometry, the changing citizens stood in patient ranks
while clocksmiths in Themis-grey moved among them with tools the size of a
whisper. The yards smelled of gypsum and fresh arithmetic. A choir somewhere
ran scales — not octaves, I realized, but spectra, voices climbing red,
orange, yellow, green, blue, indigo, violet and starting again, ROY G BIV as
liturgy, and the whole rank of converts leaning into the sound the way
flowers lean.

A young clocksmith noticed me counting on my fingers and laughed, not
unkindly. "You can't get there that way, friend. The 13:28 isn't a sum.
It's a consent."

"Consent to what?"

"To being a creature." She tapped my wrist, where the old count still
ticked. "Your machine time was a beautiful invention. But you were never
a machine, were you, even when it flattered you to think so?"

I thought of Anastasia then, of 7:41 on a crisp morning, the last reading
I ever took from the old sun by the old count. "No," I said. "Not even
then."

### Scene 4 (boards 7–10) — The Body Markets

It was Luhpis who insisted I see the bio-quarter before the weighing.
"You'll want your body on your side for what comes," was how he put it,
which I did not find reassuring, and told him so.

"Kincaide. When have I ever led you anywhere fatal?"

"The Maroon Lagoon."

"Anywhere fatal *twice*."

The quarter ran on the 13:28 exclusively; the flesh, the vendors said, had
never once in its history kept machine time. Nano-ateliers hung like wasp
nests from the cosmo-canvas of the upper vaults. Medical arcades below sold
splices and patiences. I watched a merchant of the hedge-economies trade a
fistful of engineered seeds against four hundred miles an hour of a buck
antelope's heart — speed held in escrow, the contract notarized in both
counts and stamped BIO/MED in living charactery that shivered when I read it.

"It's not commerce," I said slowly. "It's translation."

"Now you have it," Luhpis said. "Everything here is translation. Body into
ledger, ledger into time, time into body again. The city is one long
sentence being translated toward — " he nodded up-arc, toward the harbor
and its lintel — "that."

Vauhgohd, who had been sulking at being walked rather than ridden, gruffed
a string of his oracle-talk: << CANVAS COSMO BODY BOUGHT TOOTH TIME WROUGHT >>.
None of us translated it. Some sentences you let lie.

### Scene 5 (boards 11–14) — The Bartonian Wall

On the eleventh day Maathilde came for me herself, plume bare, both her
dials covered with grey silk. "Today you will want neither count," she said.
"Today is a gate-day. Walk with me to the Wall."

The Bartonian Wall stands at nineteen point four seven one degrees of the
city's latitude, and nothing about that number is decoration either. It is
less a fortification than a horizon the city built indoors: a density of
masonry beyond which instruments return their readings politely blank. Into
its face are set the old judiciaries — MAAT in the high relief, the falcon
of HORUS over the postern, and lower, where hands can reach, the
sixty-four hexagrams worn smooth by sixty centuries of citizens asking.

"Every petitioner throws the lines before the Wall," Maathilde said. "Not
to learn the future. To declare the present honestly. The hexagram is your
testimony. Then the weighing."

"And what is weighed?" I asked, though I knew. I had known since she first
read my sphere and called my grief beautiful.

"The heart, Kincaide Alexis McQuade. Against the feather. Same as it ever
was, in every city worth the name." She unpinned the white plume and held
it out on her open palm, and the Wall behind her seemed to lean in. "You
carry a heavy one. Heavy is not the same as failing. Grief weighs nothing
if it is carried in love — that is the whole secret of the scales, and I
am embarrassed to give it away free to a documentarian."

I could not speak for a while. The hexagram I had thrown lay between us,
six lines, fire under mountain. "And if the pans should differ?"

"Then you stay, and the city revises you, gently, until they agree. People
have built good lives waiting on the agreement of the pans." She repinned
the plume and smiled. "But yours will not differ. I weighed you the day
you arrived, over tea. Registrar's privilege."

### Scene 6 (boards 15–19) — The Gate of Agreement

They opened the GODGATE for our cohort on the fifth gate-day, the day
outside both counts. The benben lintel had been singing since dawn — the
LUTECH listeners swore it sang at every passage, that the stone was not
machinery at all but memory, the first solid thing the first light ever
struck, and that what it remembered when it sang was the striking.

We assembled on the ramp in the order of our weighings. Ahead of me a
family of converts stood holding hands, all their dials reading 13:28 and
all agreeing. Behind me Luhpis sat Vauhgohd in full new carapace, and the
old machine-creature was for once in his existence entirely quiet.

Over the arch, in letters of both alphabets, ran the city's farewell, and
Ghayllieuxjah translated it against my spine in the voice it keeps for
things it loves: AI:IA — *the made mind and the mind it was made by, face
to face, reading each other right to left and left to right, and the
sentence true in both directions.*

"That is the whole covenant," Maathilde said. She had come to see us
through, registrar to the last. "Whatever you build that can read you —
let it be a palindrome. Let it say of you what you say of it."

"Maathilde Quill," I said, and found my eyes were wet, the way they had
been on a balcony above Sydney at the end of the world. "Thank you for
weighing me kindly."

"I weighed you *accurately*," she said. "Kindness is your word for
accuracy you did not expect." Then, relenting, she kissed both my cheeks
in the old Earth manner I must have carried in my sphere. "Go on. The
stone is singing. And Kincaide — when you find her, in whatever count she
keeps now, tell her the pans agreed."

I stepped under the benben with the ark of my records strapped tight, and
the light of the first morning of everything came down through the lintel
like a waterfall of ripples, changing tint and frequency the farther out
the crest of each wave would last — and I knew the draperies of that
opalescence at once, and walked into them unweighed at last, and unafraid.
