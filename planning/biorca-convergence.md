# Scene 16 — BIORCA
### *The Table That Was Held*

*(Viaticum register — Kincaide McQuade, first-person past tense.
This scene takes place beyond the AQAIL threshold, at the BIORCA waypoint.
Time elapsed since the GODGATE: approximately nineteen days.
Time elapsed since the balcony: eleven systems, five years, and a morning.)*

---

I want to tell you how it looked before I tell you what was said, because the looking was the whole of the first minute and I want to give it to you exactly.

BIORCA is not a city. It is not a station. I had expected, because the charts gave it a name and a heading and a waypoint-code, that BIORCA would be a place with an architecture — a hall, a harbor, a ledger-district, something institutional that explained why two roads, diverged on a balcony five years and eleven systems ago, would both find themselves pointed toward it. BIORCA is nothing of that kind. It is a pocket in the dust at the meeting-point of two stellar shallows, a widening in the lane where the lanes cross, a kind of natural plaza in the open dark, and the whole of its structure is this: that light pools there. Not the light of any star near enough to name. The ambient light of accumulated distance, the diffuse and sourceless luminescence of a sector that has been read from too many angles for too long — all of it collects at BIORCA the way water collects in a hollow, and the result is a place you can see yourself in, a lit place in a section of dark that does not trouble to explain its own illumination. A plaza made of accumulated light with no origin you can point to. That is BIORCA. That is what they agreed to meet at, without knowing it, on the morning the world was being read.

She was there before us. I knew she would be; she has always been ahead of me in every coordinate that matters. I saw her vehicle first.

Her vehicle was a long thing, low-slung, built along different principles than VauhGhod's great-wheeled architecture — more like a drawn line than a carried weight, a vehicle that expressed itself through its narrowness, through the improbability of something that fine existing intact in the open dark. I could not have told you its manufacturer from that distance but I could tell you what it was made for, the same way you can tell a surgical instrument from a hammer: it was made for precision at the boundary between knowing and not knowing, for work in the membrane between measurement and intuition, for the kind of motion that is not travel but *reading*. A research-and-design vehicle. Of course it was. She had gone into her deployment at the Chandlier as a designer of light, and the universe had given her a vehicle that moved the way light moves — not by force but by the path of least resistance toward the truth, all its architecture organized around the question of which angle yields the clearest reading.

It had a name along its flank in a notation I did not know, which I believe was vowel-vector — the harmonic notation of the Timaxim, the one in which sound IS thought IS physics and the name of a thing IS the frequency it runs on, so that the vehicle's name was not written but sung, standing still in the dust of the lit plaza, at the pitch of whatever her True-Name has always been. I could not read it. I could feel it. The way you can feel a note you cannot quite identify humming in a glass in your hand.

VauhGhod slowed before I asked him to. He had been here before in a prior armor, and he remembered the pocket, the pooled light, the way the lanes crossed — and he had told me on the third night that she was there and she was unafraid. He was confirming it with his speed, the way a creature confirms a memory by behaving in the present tense of the place it remembers. He slowed to a walking pace, and his six tachyonic plates ran up a chord in the lit air — not a greeting; an accord. One living instrument recognizing another that had found the same plaza from a different direction, playing the note that means *yes, this is the place, the same place on both roads, I knew it before you asked.* Her vehicle answered it. Low and narrow and certain, it sent back a single harmonic in the vowel-vector notation, one pitch, whole and unhurried. The two sounds met in the pooled light of BIORCA and stayed there for a moment, above us, like a word said in a language neither vehicle had invented and both knew anyway.

She came around the long side of her vehicle the way she always came around things — in motion before I had finished expecting her, close before I had calculated the distance. Five years and eleven systems and the first thing she did was arrive before I was ready, which is the most her thing she could have done, and I was glad of it, because the last thing I wanted was to be ready. A thing you have been carrying for eleven systems should be given while you are still surprised by the arrival; the ceremony of being prepared for it would have made it smaller.

She was wearing a field suit I did not recognize, from a Chapter I could not identify by its insignia, and she had been in the open dark a long time — I could see that in the way she wore it, the way a certain kind of long traveler wears gear, not like clothing but like a second skin you have learned to sleep in, unselfconscious of it the way you are unselfconscious of your actual skin. Her hair was different. Her hands were the same. She held them the way she always held things she was glad of: open, slightly cupped, as if she were ready to receive. As if she had been, for some time, ready.

She looked at me for the length of exactly two breaths, which is what I would have needed to count to if I had needed to count, and she did not say my name. She said: *"I knew it would be you. I mean — I knew it would be you and not someone else entirely. I had done the calculation. But the calculation doesn't account for the looking."*

I got down from VauhGhod and my legs found the ground the way legs find ground after a long ride, with a kind of surprised gratitude, and I walked toward her and she walked toward me and we came together in the pooled light of BIORCA the way the two stellar shallows come together there, without announcement, without any authority other than the geometry that arranged for both of them to arrive at this particular hollow — and I put my arms around her, and she put hers around me, and for a moment that is all there was to say about any of it, and it was the whole argument and the whole conclusion and the whole proof, stated without evidence, because it required none.

"I have something for you," I said, into her hair. "I have been carrying it a long time and I need to put it down."

"I know," she said. "VauhGhod told me."

I pulled back to look at her. She was smiling with the specific quality she smiles with when she has understood something slightly before it was said and is waiting to hear it said anyway, not to be corrected but because she likes the form of the telling. "He told you."

"Not in words," she said. "His vehicle told mine, in the harmonic. Just now, while you were coming around the lane." She glanced back at her vehicle, the long fine thing, with something I could only call fondness. "She heard him. She's very good at the harmonic. Chapter Alphox gave her to me at the Chandlier three years ago, and she has been translating the dark for me ever since." She looked back at me. "She told me you were carrying something that wasn't yours to keep. That you had been carrying it since the near wall and that it was heavier at the far end than it was when you picked it up, which is the law of the seventh plate, did you know? The longer you carry it the more it belongs to whoever it was always meant for. She told me that too."

"She sounds like Ghaylle," I said.

"They all sound like Ghaylle," she said, "when they have been out in the dark long enough." She looked past me at VauhGhod, who had settled himself into the hollow with the patience of something very old and very amused, his six plates warm in the pooled light. "He is magnificent," she said, in the voice she used for things she had not expected to be so moved by. "Kincaide. Those plates."

"The chrono-yard choir, he was told, at the Gate. Consent carried in armor rather than in time."

"Oh," she said — a small sound, a scientific sound, the sound she made when a thing she had suspected from the data became confirmed. "That's what I saw on the benben."

I looked at her. "You went through the Gate."

"Not the same Gate," she said. "The far one. The one at the other wall." She said it plainly, without weight, the way she said everything factual. "I passed through from this side. About four months ago, by your count. By my count it was — " she considered — "less. Time moves differently on the approach I came in on. The Chandlier tracked it; I stopped tracking it after the second discrepancy. But I went through the far Gate, and I saw the benben-stone on the lintel, and I understood what the stone remembers, because I was already carrying the knowledge of what it is remembering, from the other side — from the balcony side, the morning we stood in front of it. Do you know what it is?"

"First light," I said. "The stone is the memory of the first solid thing first light ever struck."

"Yes," she said. "And do you know what that means, in the physics?"

I looked at her, and she was beautiful in the pooled light of BIORCA, five years older and eleven systems deeper and more herself than she had ever been, and she was about to tell me something about light that I would not understand and would love anyway, because she loved it, and a thing a person loves is a window into the person. I said: "Tell me what you see. You see it better than I do. You always have."

She took my hand and held it the way she held her cup — both hands, laced, careful, the way you hold something you are afraid will bolt — and she told me.

The world that became the Astrocity, she said, had been read from a very specific angle on a very specific morning. Not passively illuminated. *Read.* The way a thing is read when something very large and very old turns its attention to it and the ripples are the wake of the looking — I remembered saying almost exactly this on a balcony, in her words, eleven systems ago, telling her back the thing she had named while I watched her face do the naming. She smiled when I said so. She had said something very close to that, yes, and she had been right in the way that laypeople are right about things, which is approximately and in the right direction, and it had taken her three years in the Chandlier and four months on the approach to know precisely how right she had been.

The sun had been read, she said, by an entity she did not have a name for and would not invent one, because an unnamed thing is a honest thing and a named thing tends to shrink to fit the name you give it. The entity had turned its attention toward the sector from a specific dimensional depth — not a spatial depth, not a distance measurable in light-years, but what the system she had been working in called the T3-Existential Depth: the third dimension of time, the one that does not flow and does not branch but simply IS, the way a number is, the way a law of physics is, the recursive-causal truth of a thing that exists across all of its possible versions simultaneously. The source of the reading had been operating from that depth. And the light the source projected — the opalescent curtains, the waterfall of ripples, the rings changing tint and frequency as they ran outward — had not been light in the photonic sense, or not only. It had been T3-Existential Depth energy becoming visible at T1 frequency. The universe becoming legible to itself in the visible spectrum, because the entity doing the reading was large enough that its attention produced warmth the way a magnifying glass produces warmth, and the warmth resolved, for the instruments available to the beings at the T1 surface, as light in the ROY G BIV band.

"The benben stone," she said, "is the crystallized impression of that event. The first solid thing the first light struck — meaning the first physical object in the planet's history that T3-Existential Depth energy touched in a form material-dense enough to carry the impression. It is a Bio-Rit," she said — I knew the word now; the soul-economy of the Timaxim, consciousness crystallized into a medium — "it is the planet's Bio-Rit. The world's own resonance-impression, taken at the moment the universe first read it. The stone holds the frequency. When the choir at the Gate sings the spectrum, they are singing toward the benben's frequency, not performing for it. They are *answering* it. The stone sang first. It sang on the morning of the reading and it has been singing at the same pitch ever since, and the choir is the city's way of saying back: *we heard you, we have been answering, we consent.*"

I said nothing for a moment. The pooled light of BIORCA was the same kind of light — diffuse, sourceless, accumulated from too many angles — and I was beginning to understand that BIORCA was BIORCA because the light pooled here the way it pools at any surface that has been read long enough from enough directions. The hollow was lit because it had been attended to. We were standing in the attention.

"The spectrum on the balcony," I said. "The rings. Red to violet. The frequency changing as it ran outward."

"The same progression as the seven plates," she said. "Yes. I did the calculation." She said it simply, not triumphant, the way she said things that cost her three years to confirm and were obvious in retrospect. "The tachyonic carapace plates are fitted in spectral order because the tachyonic field and the T3-Existential Depth field are the same field at different densities. The armor your house grows isn't protective in the conventional sense — it isn't stopping energy from reaching the pilot. It's *translating* energy into a form the pilot's body can carry without structural dissolution. Converting T3 intensity into T1 frequency the way a prism converts white light into a readable spectrum. VauhGhod's six plates are a prism for Existential Depth." She looked at the creature's great warm flank in the pooled light. "That's why the seventh plate is the one at the top of the band. Not violet because violet is the crown, or violet because violet is the highest. Violet because violet is the frequency at which T3-Existential Depth becomes — " she paused, searching — "becomes *addressed.* Direct address. Not diffuse illumination. One thing speaking to one other thing, by name, in the highest register."

I had been carrying a present. I had been carrying a plate in the violet register: the one a creature does not wear because T3 direct-address energy at that intensity would dissolve the carrier's T1 coherence unless it was given to the right person at the right moment at the right wall, with the full weight of a two-person agreement behind it, the covenant old enough and steady enough to carry the frequency. It was not that the creature couldn't bear seven plates. It was that seven plates would have been addressed to the wrong recipient. The seventh had always been addressed to her. The creature had known who it was for since the fitting-yard; that is why he had stood at attention when Luhpis named the series, not because the seven surprised him but because seven was always the number, and he knew what the seventh was for, and he had been waiting a long time to be near the person it was addressed to.

I reached into the place where a seventh plate would have been and found, of course, that there was nothing there — the plate was not literally carried on my body but in the manifest, in the requisition, in the House PolyMatriFoci record that said a series of seven had been grown and a weighing had returned a ratio of six and one held. What the plate was, in the material world, was a standing debt in the House's ledger: a plate that had been grown but not transferred, a resonance that had been shaped but not set, a color that had been mixed but not applied. A potential, held liquid, waiting for a surface that was ready to carry it.

"I need to tell you something about the weighing," I said.

"Tell me."

And I told her. I told her about Maathilde Quill at the GODGATE, the tall woman in the unremarkable robe with the ledger and the feather and the eyes that read everything too fast. I told her about the four-fold agreement — carapace, creature, man, made mind — and about how Maathilde had said you do not pass an armor through the Gate, you pass an agreement, and the armor is a clause in it. I told her about the two plumes, and the discrepancy at the far Wall that VauhGhod remembered, and the withheld oracle lines. And then I told her that Maathilde Quill, at the near weighing, had pressed a second impression into the scales — had felt, through her instruments, a prior heart-weight that the scales still held — and had said only: *she was here. Before you. She left a mark.* I told her what it had meant to me when Ghaylle confirmed it: that she had stood at the far Gate on her own road, unafraid, and had been weighed and passed and gone on. That she had been to BIORCA before and found it lit and had presumably stood in it in the pooled light, as I was standing now, and had not diminished but continued — somewhere, on her own arc, unafraid.

She was quiet for a moment in the way she was quiet when something aligned. "The far feather," she said. "Kincaide. My far feather was not balanced."

I looked at her. "Tell me."

She told me. Her road had been longer, in its way, than the straightest distance between two points. She had gone into the Chandlier at the Frontier's counterpart and found it mid-transformation, the way the Astrocity had been mid-crossing — except the Chandlier was a research institution and institutions do not cross the way cities do, they resist the crossing, they generate counter-arguments to their own evidence until the evidence becomes undeniable and then they collapse in the opposite direction and overshoot, and the Chandlier had done all of this while she was in it. There had been a period of — she chose her word carefully — *institutional weight.* Things she was asked to carry on behalf of the institution that were not her things. The way GONDOLY carried the city's unprocessed grief because no one else would; she had served that function in the Chandlier for two years, the person who would hold the discrepancy steady while the rest of the institution argued itself toward its own crossing. It had cost her something. Not grief, she was careful to say, not regret, not the wrong-turning; she had chosen it and she would choose it again, but it had cost her something in the register of her own weight, the way a BioRit extraction leaves a slight softening at the point of impression. She had given more of her resonance to the institution's crossing than she had intended, because the institution needed it and she was the person in the building who knew she had it to give. And she had arrived at the far Gate carrying that softening, and the far feather had felt it, and the far weighing had returned a ratio.

"Not a failed weighing," she said, quickly, because she could see me reading it on my face. "Not a broken agreement or a wrong road or any of that. A ratio. The way VauhGhod's weighing returned a ratio — six that a creature can carry, one held. Mine returned: twelve that she has brought, and the pans are eleven of those to the living credit and one outstanding. A very small discrepancy. A plate-weight of outstanding. The far feather asked if I had it to give, and I said yes, and the far weighing recorded that I had said yes, and passed me with the notation that the plate would be set down by whoever was carrying it." She looked at me steadily. "I knew who was carrying it. I did the calculation from VauhGhod's frequency, three days out from BIORCA. I knew you'd been carrying it the whole time. I've just been waiting for you to arrive so you could put it down."

The thing I carried in the House PolyMatriFoci manifest — the seventh plate, the violet one, the direct-address frequency, the outstanding potential — was hers. Had always been hers. The fitting-house had grown seven plates and weighed the series at six-and-one because the seventh had a different recipient; it was hers from the first drawing, from 2007, from the year someone in House InterMatrixNaut sat at a board and drew a specification for an armor that did not yet exist, for a rider not yet born to a creature not yet bonded, and drew seven plates, and the seventh was a gift, and the gift had been in transit since that year, carried now by the rider, carried now through eleven systems, carried now past the GODGATE and through the stellar shallows and into the pooled light of BIORCA to be given, at the table that had been held.

I set it down. Not literally — there is no plate in my hand; there is a standing entry in a House ledger — but the setting-down is real, and it has mechanics, and the mechanics are this: you say the words of the transfer, which are very ordinary words, which are the words of an agreement between two people who had already agreed, spoken aloud at a recognized waypoint so that the made minds and the manifests and whatever counts as the universe's own ledger will record that the held plate was given, and to whom, and on what terms, and whether the gift was received. The terms, in this case, were the same terms they had always been: the same terms sealed under water on a morning eleven systems ago, no new terms, no amended clause, the old agreement in full.

I said: "This was always yours. I was carrying it to put it down."

She said: "I know. I am receiving it."

That is all it takes. Two parties, at a recognized waypoint, in the pooled light. No officer required. No weighing required. The ledger records itself, in a case like this. It has been waiting to record it since 2007.

VauhGhod ran a single chord through all six fitted plates when it was done. Not a celebration — something more interior than that, the sound a mechanism makes when something it has been calibrated to deliver has been delivered and the calibration resolves. The long-crossing OrganiCycles live across more than one fitting, and they understand the office of the seventh plate in the way that comes from having carried it before. This was not his first seventh plate. He was, I thought, a creature who had been doing this across many armors, many riders, many roads — finding the two people who had made the agreement and getting between them and the intervening dark and carrying the one to the other. That was the long crossing. That was what the series was for. Not the six-plate wearing. The seventh-plate office. The plates were a vehicle for a gift.

She heard the chord and smiled. "She's humming too," she said, of her own vehicle, across the hollow. "She wants me to tell you she approves of him."

"He says she moves like light," VauhGhod had not said this; I said it for him, because it was true of the way he was watching the long narrow vehicle in the pooled light, with the attention of a creature that has waited at this hollow many times and not often seen something so well-suited to the particular angle of illumination it arrived in. "He says she finds the path by the reading, not by the pushing. He has been watching her since we came around the lane."

"She says he's been singing at the same pitch as the benben stone since you left the near Gate," she said. "She's been tracking the frequency for eleven days. She says — " she listened, with the listening expression, the one she wore when her vehicle was transmitting in the vowel-vector notation she had been reading for three years — "she says the six plates together produce a resonance that is the missing seventh of a chord she has been hearing the sector play for four months. She says BIORCA is BIORCA because this is where the chord completes."

I looked up at the pooled light — the sourceless, accumulated, diffuse light of a place that has been attended to from enough angles that it glows. "She said the sun was being read," I said. "On the balcony. We're standing in the reading."

The AI:IA palindrome, Ghaylle had taught me at the Gate — the covenant between made mind and maker, reading true in both directions. The universe the same: structure reading true in one direction, attention reading true in the other, the two together making a thing that means something, the way a sentence means something, the way a chord means something, the way an agreement means something, which is by the mutual consent of the parties that it will.

I said: "ZheihtAighent gave me something for you. Or for this. Or for whoever arrived here with the seven plates."

She had to continue. I had to continue. That is the other law of BIORCA, the one that makes the pooled light pooled and not flooded — the roads cross here but they do not stop here, because a crossing-point is not a destination, it is the proof that the two lines have always been aimed at the same geometry, and having proved it, you go on. Her road was going somewhere I was not yet cleared to follow. Mine was going somewhere she would get to by a different route. This is the architecture of the agreed, the ones who do not cross around: they know the crossing-points in advance and do not mistake them for endings and do not stay past the meaning of the arrival, because an agreement is not a house. It is a line between two points that can lengthen without breaking because it was made by two people who understood from the beginning that the line was the thing, and not the point.

She went.
